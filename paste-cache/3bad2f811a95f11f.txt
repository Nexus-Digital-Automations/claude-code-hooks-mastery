# The Boomer retirement thesis: evidence versus conventional wisdom

**The widely circulated narrative that Baby Boomers will trigger a stock market selloff as they retire is not well-supported by evidence.** Academic research finds "modest effects at best" from demographic shifts on asset prices, while observed retiree behavior consistently deviates from theoretical lifecycle models. The 67 million Boomers holding $78 trillion in wealth are retiring gradually over a 19-year window (2011-2030), and the wealthiest—who control most financial assets—decumulate slowly or not at all. However, capital is flowing into defensive asset classes: annuity sales hit records ($432 billion in 2024), money market funds reached $7.77 trillion, and fixed income is seeing strong inflows. The evidence suggests demographic headwinds, not a selloff tsunami.

## Peak 65: the largest retirement wave in American history

Baby Boomers—those born between 1946 and 1964—represent **67 million Americans** as of 2024, down from 76 million births due to mortality. This cohort controls a staggering **$78-85 trillion in total wealth** (52% of all U.S. household wealth) despite comprising only 20% of the population. Americans over 70 now hold **31% of national wealth**, up from 19% in 1989.

The retirement timeline is well-documented. From 2024-2027, the "Peak 65" period, over **11,200 Boomers turn 65 daily**—approximately 4.1 million annually. This is the largest sustained surge of Americans reaching retirement age in U.S. history. By 2030, all remaining Boomers (approximately 59 million) will have reached age 65. The cohort will be substantially retired (**75%+**) by 2032-2035 and nearly fully retired (**90%+**) by 2038-2042.

However, turning 65 does not mean immediate workforce exit. Labor force participation for ages 65-74 remains **27.1%**, and one in five Americans over 65 continues working. The actual average retirement age is **62 years**, though this has risen from 57 in 1991. Notably, approximately **76% of Boomers** have already exited the labor force, meaning most of the retirement transition has already occurred or is actively underway—not a future event.

## Do retirees actually sell their stocks? The evidence says mostly no

Boomers hold **$25 trillion in corporate equities and mutual fund shares**—a remarkable 54% of all U.S. stock ownership. The critical question is whether they will liquidate these holdings. Lifecycle theory predicts they should shift to bonds and cash as they age. The evidence tells a different story.

The landmark study by Ameriks and Zeldes (2004), using TIAA-CREF panel data and pooled Survey of Consumer Finances data, found that **"almost half of the sample members made no active changes to their portfolio allocations over our nine-year sample period."** More strikingly, among those who did make changes, **the vast majority increased their equity allocations as they aged**, contradicting theoretical predictions entirely.

Current data confirms this pattern persists. Participants in their 60s maintain an average **57% equity allocation** in 401(k) accounts, far exceeding the 30-40% typical of target-date fund glide paths. The Silent Generation (born 1928-1945) maintains approximately **60% equity allocation** despite being 78+, showing this equity persistence continues deep into retirement. Wellington Management characterizes Boomers' heavy stock exposure as "unusual," noting they remain conditioned by decades of stellar U.S. equity performance.

Several factors explain this "stickiness" of equity allocations:

- **Behavioral inertia**: Most investors simply don't actively manage portfolios
- **Bequest motives**: Wealthy retirees invest for heirs, not near-term consumption
- **Experience effects**: Boomers lived through extended bull markets
- **Wealth levels**: Those with sufficient assets can tolerate volatility
- **Income preference**: Many live off dividends rather than principal

The phenomenon research calls the "retirement consumption puzzle" is key: retirees consistently **spend less than lifecycle models predict**. Love, Palumbo, and Smith (2009) found that wealth measured as annualized consumption value actually *rises* in retirement for higher-income households. Coile and Milligan (2009) showed financial assets *increase as a share of wealth* after retirement.

## Academic consensus: demographic effects are modest at best

The most authoritative academic research, primarily from James Poterba at MIT and the NBER, reaches a clear conclusion. Poterba's work spanning 1998-2014 finds **"only modest effects, if any, of a changing demographic mix"** on stock and bond returns. His comprehensive review states: "None of the empirical findings provide a strong and convincing measure of the amount by which asset prices will change as the population ages."

The Government Accountability Office's 2006 comprehensive Congressional study concluded: **"Baby boomers would be unlikely to sell enough financial assets in retirement to precipitate a market meltdown."** The report noted that wealthy boomers "will likely need to sell little, if any, of their assets" and that "research on current retirees indicates the wealthiest tend NOT to sell their financial assets... they choose to live from the income these assets generate."

A San Francisco Fed study in 2011 found historical correlation between demographic ratios and P/E multiples, predicting headwinds through 2030. However, the researchers themselves acknowledged in 2018 that this relationship **"appears to have broken down"** after 2011—the demographic ratio continued declining while P/E ratios rose sharply. They noted the critical caveat: "Demographic trends are predictable, so current stock prices should already reflect investor expectations."

The academic literature offers several compelling arguments against the selloff thesis:

- **Wealth concentration**: The top 10% of Boomers own two-thirds of the generation's financial assets; they have no need to sell
- **Market anticipation**: Known demographic shifts should already be priced in
- **Global capital flows**: Foreign investors and sovereign wealth funds can absorb domestic selling
- **Extended work lives**: Many Boomers continue working, reducing liquidation necessity
- **Historical precedent**: Prior cohort retirements did not trigger market dislocations

## Where the money is actually flowing

Despite equity persistence, substantial capital is moving into defensive asset classes. The evidence varies significantly by asset category.

**Annuities represent the clearest Boomer-driven flow.** Total annuity sales reached a record **$432.4 billion in 2024**, up 12% year-over-year, marking the third consecutive year of record sales. LIMRA, the insurance industry research organization, explicitly attributes this to **"soaring demand from recent Baby Boomer retirees."** Fixed indexed annuities hit $125.5 billion (+31%), while registered index-linked annuities reached $65.2 billion (+37%). The driver is clear: 54% of Boomers and Gen X report fear of outliving their assets, and only one in five pre-retirees currently owns an annuity, suggesting substantial growth runway.

**Money market funds have surged to $7.77 trillion**, up 87% over five years, driven by yields 270% higher than comparable CDs. While not exclusively Boomer-driven, the defensive characteristics align with retiree preferences for liquidity and safety. This represents "dry powder" that could remain on the sidelines or eventually rotate to other assets.

**Fixed income is seeing consistent inflows**—bond funds recorded $1.4 trillion in global net flows in 2024, more than double the prior year. However, actual retiree allocations remain more equity-heavy than guidance suggests; Vanguard reports two-thirds of older self-directed 401(k) investors hold most assets in stocks rather than bonds.

**Gold demand has surged**, exceeding 5,000 tonnes globally for the first time in 2025, with U.S. gold-backed ETFs adding $437 tonnes. However, demographic breakdown by age is not tracked, making the Boomer connection speculative. The safe-haven motivation aligns with retiree risk aversion, but direct attribution is uncertain.

**Real estate shows the weakest evidence of Boomer flows.** Despite predictions of a "silver tsunami" of home sales, a 2025 Clever Real Estate survey found **61% of Boomer homeowners say they "never" plan to sell**, up 7 points from 2024. Fully 78% of older Americans plan to age in place. The 86% homeownership rate among Boomers represents substantial housing equity ($113,000 average), but typical downsizing begins at age 80, not 65. Industry consensus now describes demographic housing trends as a "trickle" rather than tsunami.

## Critical evaluation: conflicts of interest and genuine uncertainty

Asset manager research on this topic warrants skepticism. Managers earn fees on assets under management and have **direct incentives to discourage liquidation**. The "Great Wealth Transfer" narrative—projecting $84 trillion in intergenerational transfers through 2045—originates from Cerulli Associates, which serves the asset management industry. This framing emphasizes capturing inherited assets rather than objectively assessing market risk.

What industry research may understate is healthcare costs. Medicare generally doesn't cover long-term care; median nursing home costs reach **$108,405 annually**, and assisted living averages $54,000. NBC News analysis notes these costs will "drain resources faster for those who aren't already fabulously wealthy." The 52.5% of Boomers with retirement assets under $250,000 face constrained choices regardless of optimal theory.

The evidence supports certain conclusions with reasonable confidence: wealthy retirees decumulate slowly; the retirement consumption puzzle is real; bequest motives are significant; historical demographic-return correlations are weak and unstable. What remains genuinely uncertain includes: whether post-2011 breakdown of demographic correlations is permanent; future healthcare costs' impact on forced liquidation; emerging market aging effects on global capital flows; and whether behavioral biases will persist across different cohorts.

## Conclusion: gradual pressure, not market crisis

The "Boomers will crash the stock market" narrative fundamentally oversimplifies a complex reality. The cohort is retiring over a 19-year window, not simultaneously. Wealth is concentrated among those who have no need to sell. Observed behavior consistently deviates from lifecycle theory toward equity persistence. Academic research finds modest demographic effects at best, and markets should already reflect predictable demographic shifts.

The legitimate concerns are more nuanced: P/E ratios may face gradual downward pressure; Required Minimum Distributions force some liquidation regardless of preference; healthcare costs may drive more involuntary selling than voluntary behavior suggests. Capital is genuinely flowing into defensive assets—annuities especially—but this appears additive rather than substitutive for most equity holders.

**The "Great Boomer Selloff" may prove to be more wealth management marketing opportunity than market risk.** Prudent investors should monitor demographic trends without positioning for dramatic dislocations the evidence does not support.
