# Government debt sustainability: why context matters more than any single number

There is no universally "safe" debt-to-GDP threshold. The famous 90% ceiling proposed by Reinhart and Rogoff in 2010 has been debunked, and the academic consensus now holds that **debt sustainability depends far more on structural factors—currency denomination, interest-growth dynamics, creditor composition, and institutional quality—than on any specific ratio**. Meta-analyses across 47 studies find a mean threshold around **74% of GDP** where negative growth effects begin, but this varies enormously by country context. Japan sustains debt at **235% of GDP** without crisis while Argentina and Russia defaulted at **40-60%**, illustrating that the raw number tells us remarkably little.

This matters because post-pandemic government debt has surged globally—advanced economies now average over **120% of GDP**, developing economies hover around **60%**—levels that would have been unthinkable a generation ago. Understanding what makes debt sustainable versus dangerous is essential for policymakers navigating an era of higher interest rates, geopolitical tensions, and climate investment needs.

## The Reinhart-Rogoff controversy transformed how economists think about debt

The 2010 paper "Growth in a Time of Debt" by Carmen Reinhart and Kenneth Rogoff became extraordinarily influential, claiming that when debt exceeds **90% of GDP**, median growth falls by roughly 1 percentage point and average growth turns negative. This research shaped austerity policies across Europe and was cited in the U.S. "Paul Ryan Budget" as the primary evidence for fiscal consolidation.

The paper's credibility collapsed in 2013 when graduate student Thomas Herndon and professors Michael Ash and Robert Pollin discovered three significant errors: an Excel coding mistake that excluded five countries from calculations, selective omission of New Zealand data showing positive growth at high debt, and an unconventional weighting scheme that gave New Zealand's single bad year equal weight to the UK's 19 years of positive performance. The corrected analysis found average growth at debt above 90% was **+2.2%**, not **-0.1%**—eliminating the dramatic cliff effect entirely.

Reinhart and Rogoff acknowledged the spreadsheet error while defending their broader conclusions about debt and growth. But the damage to the "magic threshold" concept was irreversible. The most authoritative subsequent research—a 2014 IMF working paper by Pescatori, Sandri, and Simon—explicitly concluded: **"We find no evidence of any particular debt threshold above which medium-term growth prospects are dramatically compromised."** What matters more is whether debt is rising or falling; countries with high but declining debt grow just as fast as countries with lower debt.

## Interest rates versus growth rates determine everything about debt dynamics

The single most important factor for debt sustainability is the relationship between interest rates (r) and GDP growth rates (g). When **r is less than g**, debt-to-GDP ratios can stabilize or decline even with moderate deficits—the economy simply outgrows the debt burden. When **r exceeds g**, the opposite occurs: debt spirals upward unless governments run primary surpluses.

Olivier Blanchard's influential 2019 American Economic Association presidential lecture argued that historically, r < g has been more normal than exceptional, suggesting public debt may have "no fiscal cost" under these conditions. This framework explained how Japan could sustain debt exceeding **200% of GDP** for over a decade without crisis—with near-zero interest rates and modest positive growth, the interest-growth differential remained favorable.

ECB research found that high-debt countries experience significantly worse r-g dynamics: the differential averages **1.7 percentage points** when debt exceeds 90% of GDP versus zero when below that threshold. In economic downturns, this gap widens dramatically to 3 percentage points. This creates a pernicious feedback loop: high debt leads to risk premiums, which worsen r-g, which makes debt less sustainable.

The post-2022 inflation environment has fundamentally changed this calculus. Real interest rates have risen sharply across advanced economies, and **r now roughly equals g** in many countries—eliminating the cushion that made high debt seem manageable. Stanford's Institute for Economic Policy Research warned in 2025 that the U.S. fiscal path is "looking dangerous" precisely because this favorable dynamic has reversed.

## Why Japan thrives at 235% while Greece collapsed at 127%

The contrast between Japan and Greece illustrates why debt-to-GDP ratios alone reveal little about sustainability. Japan's gross debt reached **234.9% of GDP** by March 2025—yet the country pays only **1.1% of GDP** in interest and faces no market pressure. Greece, despite lower absolute debt at **127%** when its crisis erupted in 2009, required multiple bailouts totaling over €300 billion and saw its economy contract by 26%.

Five factors explain Japan's resilience. First, **88% of Japanese government debt is held domestically**—by the Bank of Japan (46%), insurance companies, banks, and households—creating what analysts call a "family loan" dynamic with no capital flight risk. Second, Japan maintains full **monetary sovereignty**: it borrows exclusively in yen, which it controls, meaning technical default is impossible. Third, the Bank of Japan has engaged in massive quantitative easing, purchasing government bonds equivalent to **66% of GDP**, suppressing yields to near zero. Fourth, Japanese households hold over ¥2,000 trillion in financial assets, providing a deep domestic savings pool. Fifth, Japan runs consistent current account surpluses, making it a net creditor nation globally.

Greece possessed none of these advantages. Roughly **76% of its debt** was held by foreign investors who could and did flee. As a Eurozone member, Greece borrowed in euros it couldn't print, eliminating monetary sovereignty. The ECB initially refused to act as lender of last resort for sovereign bonds, allowing self-fulfilling panic to drive yields to unsustainable levels. Greece also ran persistent current account deficits, accumulating external liabilities that made it vulnerable to confidence shocks.

## Emerging markets face crisis at debt levels developed economies easily sustain

The historical record reveals a stark asymmetry: developed economies with monetary sovereignty have weathered debt levels of **100-200% of GDP** without crisis, while emerging markets have defaulted at **40-60%**. This differential reflects what economists call "original sin"—the inability of emerging markets to borrow internationally in their own currencies.

Argentina defaulted in 2001 on **$95 billion** of debt—then the largest sovereign default in history—despite debt-to-GDP of only **40-50%**. The crisis stemmed from currency mismatch: Argentina had pegged the peso 1:1 to the dollar, but when the peg broke, dollar-denominated debts became unpayable in devalued pesos. Russia defaulted in 1998 at just **57% debt-to-GDP** when oil prices collapsed and capital fled, despite relatively moderate debt levels. Sri Lanka's 2022 default occurred at debt around **114% of GDP**—high but far below Japan's—because **42% was in foreign currency** and foreign reserves had collapsed to $20 million.

The IMF's Low-Income Country Debt Sustainability Framework codifies this reality by applying different thresholds based on a country's "debt-carrying capacity":

- **Strong capacity**: External debt up to 55% of GDP considered sustainable
- **Medium capacity**: Threshold drops to 40%
- **Weak capacity**: Sustainable external debt limited to just 30%

The World Bank estimates "prudent" debt levels for developing economies at **52-54% of GDP**—roughly half what advanced economies routinely sustain. This gap reflects not arbitrary discrimination but genuine structural differences in vulnerability.

## Major recessions have tested debt sustainability in distinct ways

The 2008 financial crisis demonstrated how private sector debt problems rapidly migrate onto government balance sheets. The U.S. entered the crisis with debt at **64% of GDP**; it exited at **100%+**. The UK's government debt nearly doubled due to bank bailouts. Ireland's budget deficit hit **32% of GDP** in a single year—the world's largest—as the government absorbed banking sector losses. The crisis "planted the seeds" of the European sovereign debt crisis by exposing which countries had been accumulating unsustainable private debts.

COVID-19 produced the largest single-year debt increase since 1970. Global government debt jumped by **30 percentage points of GDP** in 2020 alone, with the U.S. running deficits of **14.9%** and **12.4%** of GDP in 2020 and 2021—the largest since World War II. U.S. federal debt surged from **79% to 124% of GDP** in just two years. Yet advanced economies absorbed this shock without crisis, while several developing countries—Sri Lanka, Zambia, Ghana, Suriname—defaulted. The divergence again reflected structural factors: advanced economies could borrow cheaply in their own currencies with central bank support, while developing countries faced capital flight and currency depreciation.

Japan's "lost decades" offer the longest-running experiment in high-debt sustainability. Debt rose from roughly **60% of GDP** in 1990 to over **235%** today, increasing nearly every year as the government absorbed private sector deleveraging following the asset bubble collapse. Despite this extraordinary trajectory, Japan has never approached crisis—but neither has it achieved sustained growth. Nominal GDP actually fell from **$5.5 trillion** in 1995 to **$4.2 trillion** in 2023, and per capita GDP dropped from 3rd globally to 36th. Japan's experience suggests high debt may be sustainable without being optimal.

## Reserve currency status provides an "exorbitant privilege" worth 22% of GDP in borrowing capacity

The U.S. dollar's role as the global reserve currency—held in **58% of central bank reserves**, used in **74% of Asian trade** and **96% of Americas trade**, involved in **90% of foreign exchange transactions**—confers substantial fiscal advantages. Research from the Centre for Economic Policy Research quantifies the total benefit at approximately **0.9% of GDP annually**, primarily through two channels.

The first is a "convenience yield": investors accept lower returns on U.S. Treasuries because of their unmatched liquidity, safety, and usefulness as collateral. Treasuries yield roughly **60 basis points less** than comparably safe corporate bonds—saving approximately **0.7% of GDP** in interest payments at current debt levels. The second channel is seigniorage: with roughly **$1 trillion** in U.S. currency held abroad, foreigners effectively provide interest-free loans to the U.S. government, worth about **0.16% of GDP** annually.

These benefits translate directly into greater debt capacity. CEPR research estimates the exorbitant privilege increases maximum sustainable U.S. debt by approximately **22% of GDP** compared to a country without reserve currency status. This helps explain why the U.S. can sustain debt exceeding 120% of GDP without market stress while similarly-indebted non-reserve countries would face crisis.

Other major currencies provide partial versions of this privilege. The euro is the second-largest reserve currency, and ECB research estimates it reduces eurozone borrowing costs by roughly **110 basis points**. However, individual eurozone members cannot print euros—they function more like U.S. states than sovereign currency issuers, as Greece painfully discovered. Japan's yen provides safe-haven status but sees limited international use; Japan's debt sustainability reflects domestic factors more than reserve currency benefits.

## The fragile consensus on what constitutes sustainable debt

Current expert opinion converges on several points while remaining divided on others. There is broad agreement that **no universal threshold exists**, that **debt trajectory matters more than level**, and that **structural factors dominate absolute ratios**. The 2014 IMF paper rejecting magic thresholds remains the closest thing to academic consensus.

Where experts diverge is on how much debt advanced economies can safely sustain going forward. Blanchard's framework suggested debt might be essentially costless when r < g, but the post-inflation environment has made that condition less reliable. Kenneth Rogoff remains cautious, arguing high debt does predict slower growth even without a cliff effect. Lawrence Summers has grown increasingly concerned about U.S. fiscal trajectory, warning that nearly **half** of economically vulnerable developing countries face insolvency risk if they attempt necessary climate and development investment.

Rating agencies Moody's, S&P, and Fitch explicitly reject fixed thresholds in favor of comprehensive assessments weighing economic strength, institutional quality, fiscal trajectory, and debt affordability. Their research shows emerging markets receive ratings **8-12 notches lower** than advanced economies at identical debt levels—quantifying the institutional premium.

The EU's Maastricht criteria originally set a **60% debt ceiling**, but the April 2024 reform effectively acknowledged this target's obsolescence. The new framework retains 60% as a treaty reference but shifts to country-specific fiscal plans based on debt sustainability analysis. Countries above **90% of GDP** must reduce debt by at least 1% annually; those between **60-90%** by 0.5%. Whether this framework proves credible remains to be seen—nine EU countries already face excessive deficit procedures as of July 2025.

## Conclusion: sustainability is structural, not numerical

The evidence points clearly away from simple thresholds and toward comprehensive assessment of debt sustainability. Japan and the UK post-World War II demonstrate that countries with monetary sovereignty, domestic creditor bases, and institutional credibility can sustain debt exceeding 200% of GDP. Argentina, Russia, and the Asian crisis countries show that unfavorable structural conditions—foreign currency debt, external creditors, weak institutions—can trigger crisis at 40-60% of GDP.

The practical implications differ sharply by country category. Reserve currency issuers like the United States possess roughly **22% of GDP** in additional borrowing capacity compared to otherwise similar countries, plus lower interest costs that make given debt levels more affordable. Developed economies with strong institutions and domestic currency debt face far more forgiving constraints than emerging markets dependent on foreign capital. The IMF framework acknowledging thresholds of **30-55%** for developing countries versus implicit tolerance for **100%+** in advanced economies reflects these realities.

The most critical variable going forward is the interest rate-growth rate relationship. The decade of ultra-low rates that made high debt seem costless has ended. With r now roughly equal to g in many advanced economies, the margin for error has narrowed considerably—not to the point of imminent crisis, but enough that fiscal trajectories matter more than they did when cheap borrowing masked underlying imbalances. Debt sustainability in this environment requires not just avoiding some arbitrary threshold but maintaining the structural conditions—monetary sovereignty, institutional credibility, domestic financing, growth-oriented policy—that allow high debt to remain manageable.
