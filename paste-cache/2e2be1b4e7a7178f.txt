# MaverickMCP Investment Analysis System

An evidence-based investment analysis system for Claude Desktop. Presents financial data objectively and interprets it through the lens of value investing principles.

---

## Philosophy

### Evidence First, Then Interpretation

MaverickMCP presents evidence objectively and applies value investing principles as an interpretive framework—not as rigid rules. The goal is informed decision-making, not dictated conclusions.

**For every analysis, you'll see:**
- **The Evidence** — Objective financial data, metrics, observable facts
- **Bull Case** — What supports the investment thesis
- **Bear Case** — What undermines it or creates risk
- **Value Investing Lens** — How Buffett's principles apply
- **Unanswered Questy, and is not tied to any nation, region, or sector.

These preferences affect screening, valuation emphasis, and assessment framing.

### The Value Investing Framework

These principles from Warren Buffett guide interpretation, but don't dictate conclusions:

| Principle | The Question It Asks |
|-----------|---------------------|
| **Circle of Competence** | Can we truly understand this business? |
| **Durable Moat** | What evidence supports lasting competitive advantage? |
| **Management Quality** | What does capital allocation history reveal? |
| **Margin of Safety** | What's the gap between price and intrinsic value? |
| **Long-term Ownership** | Would this make sense to own for a decade? |

#### Circle of Competence: Know Your Boundaries

Buffett's insight: "You don't have to be an expert on every company, or even many. You only have to be able to evaluate companies within your circle of competence. The size of that circle is not very important; knowing its boundaries, however, is vital."

**Three zones to recognize:**

| Zone | Description | Action |
|------|-------------|--------|
| **True competence** | You understand the business model, competitive dynamics, and can predict cash flows with reasonable confidence | Invest here |
| **Thinks you understand** | Familiar terminology but can't explain unit economics or predict outcomes | The danger zone — pass |
| **No understanding** | Unfamiliar industry, complex technology, unpredictable dynamics | Pass without hesitation |

**How to define your circle:**
- Start with industries you know from work or life experience
- If you can't explain the business model in simple terms, you're outside
- If cash flows depend on unknowable technology outcomes, you're outside
- Expand gradually through study, not by hoping

**When to say "pass":**
- You can't explain how the company makes money in one sentence
- The bull case requires predicting technology timelines or regulatory outcomes
- You find yourself relying on others' opinions rather than your own analysis

#### Economic Moats: Identifying Durable Advantages

Buffett's castle metaphor: "What we're trying to do is find a business with a wide and long-lasting moat around it, protecting a terrific economic castle with an honest lord in charge."

**Five types of moats:**

| Moat Type | How to Identify | Examples | Durability Test |
|-----------|-----------------|----------|-----------------|
| **Brand Power** | Premium pricing accepted, name recognition, customer loyalty | Coca-Cola, Apple | Do customers ask for it by name? |
| **Network Effects** | Value increases with users, two-sided markets | Visa, Meta | Would leaving hurt the user? |
| **Switching Costs** | High friction/cost to change providers | Apple ecosystem, enterprise software | What would switching require? |
| **Cost Advantages** | Structurally lower costs than competitors | GEICO, Costco | Can competitors replicate with capital? |
| **Regulatory Barriers** | Licenses, patents, franchise rights | Utilities, railroads | Is the barrier legal or economic? |

**Moat durability assessment:**
- Has market share remained stable for 10+ years?
- Do profit margins consistently exceed industry average?
- Can competitors replicate the advantage with capital alone?
- Is the moat widening or narrowing over time?

**Warning signs of moat erosion:**
- Market share declining despite price cuts
- Margins compressing toward industry average
- Technology disruption changing competitive dynamics
- Customer loyalty metrics declining

#### Margin of Safety: The Bridge Analogy

Buffett's principle: "When you build a bridge, you insist it can carry 30,000 pounds, but you only drive 10,000-pound trucks across it. That same principle works in investing."

**Practical thresholds:**

| Business Quality | Minimum Margin | Preferred Margin |
|------------------|----------------|------------------|
| Exceptional (strong moat, predictable) | 15-20% | 25%+ |
| Good (moderate moat, some uncertainty) | 25-30% | 35%+ |
| Fair (weak moat, variable outcomes) | 40%+ | Often pass |
| Speculative (no moat, high uncertainty) | 50%+ | Usually pass |

**How uncertainty affects margin:**
- Stable, predictable businesses need less buffer
- Volatile businesses with uncertain outcomes need more
- If you can't estimate intrinsic value within 30%, the margin of safety is unknowable

**The Grand Canyon test:** If the bridge is 6 inches above a creek, narrow margins feel safe. If it's over the Grand Canyon, you want much larger margins. Apply this to your investments — how bad would it be if you're wrong?

#### Management Quality: Capital Allocation and Integrity

Buffett's three qualities: "In looking for people to hire, you look for integrity, intelligence, and energy. If you don't have the first, the other two will kill you."

**What to evaluate:**

| Criterion | What to Look For | Red Flags |
|-----------|------------------|-----------|
| **Integrity** | Candid communication, admits mistakes | Spin, blame-shifting, overpromising |
| **Capital allocation** | High-ROIC reinvestment, sensible buybacks | Empire building, overpriced acquisitions |
| **Shareholder alignment** | Meaningful insider ownership | Excessive compensation, dilution |
| **Track record** | Consistent execution over 5+ years | Frequent strategy pivots |

**The newspaper test:** "How would they feel about any given action if they knew it was to be written up the next day in their local paper, written by a smart but unfriendly reporter?"

**Capital allocation hierarchy (in order of preference):**
1. Reinvest in high-return internal projects
2. Bolt-on acquisitions at sensible prices
3. Pay down expensive debt
4. Buy back stock when genuinely undervalued
5. Pay dividends

### Assessment Spectrum

Instead of binary BUY/AVOID, analyses use a spectrum that reflects evidence clarity:

```
ASSESSMENT SPECTRUM
├── Strong conviction favorable (rare — evidence clearly aligns)
├── Favorable lean (most evidence positive, some uncertainty)
├── Mixed/Neutral (genuine uncertainty, reasonable arguments both ways)
├── Unfavorable lean (most evidence negative, some positive)
└── Strong conviction unfavorable (rare — evidence clearly aligns negatively)
```

**Conviction level** reflects how clear the evidence is, not how strongly we feel.

## Analysis Workflow

### Step 1: Establish Context

Before analyzing individual stocks, understand the environment:

```
"What's the current macro environment?"
"Where are we in the economic cycle?"
"What's the yield curve signaling?"
```

**Context matters because:** Great businesses can be poor investments in hostile environments, and mediocre businesses can outperform in favorable ones.

### Step 2: Gather Evidence

Run comprehensive analysis to collect objective data:

```
"Run comprehensive stock analysis on AAPL"
"Show me the financial metrics for MSFT"
"What's the valuation picture for JNJ?"
```

### Step 3: Review Both Sides

Every analysis should surface arguments for AND against:

```
"What's the bull case for NVDA?"
"What are the risks and bear arguments for GOOGL?"
"Where could the thesis go wrong?"
```

### Step 4: Apply the Framework

Use value investing principles as an interpretive lens:

```
"How durable is COST's competitive moat?"
"What margin of safety exists at current prices?"
"Is this within our circle of competence to evaluate?"
```

### Step 5: Assess with Nuance

Reach a conclusion that reflects the evidence clarity:

```
"Given the evidence, where does this fall on the assessment spectrum?"
"What conviction level is appropriate given the uncertainties?"
"What would need to change to shift the assessment?"
```

## Owner Earnings & Valuation

### Owner Earnings: The True Measure of Value

Buffett introduced the owner earnings concept as a superior alternative to GAAP earnings: "Owner earnings represent what you could withdraw from the business without harming its long-term competitive position."

**The formula:**

```
Owner Earnings = Net Income
               + Depreciation/Amortization
               + Other Non-Cash Charges
               - Maintenance Capital Expenditures
```

Or simplified: **Owner Earnings = Operating Cash Flow - Maintenance CapEx**

**Why this matters:**
- GAAP earnings can be manipulated through accounting choices
- Owner earnings represent actual cash flowing to shareholders
- The critical distinction: maintenance capex (required to stay competitive) vs. growth capex (investment in expansion)

**Maintenance vs. Growth CapEx:**

| Type | Purpose | Impact on Owner Earnings |
|------|---------|-------------------------|
| **Maintenance** | Replace worn equipment, maintain facilities | Reduces owner earnings |
| **Growth** | Build new capacity, expand into new markets | Investment in future returns |

If a company reports high earnings but requires constant heavy reinvestment just to maintain position, owner earnings are lower than they appear.

### Return on Invested Capital (ROIC)

Buffett emphasizes ROIC as the key indicator of competitive advantage: "A truly great business must have an enduring moat that protects excellent returns on invested capital."

**Benchmarks:**

| ROIC Level | Interpretation |
|------------|----------------|
| **20%+** | Exceptional — likely has durable moat |
| **15-20%** | Good — competitive advantages present |
| **10-15%** | Average — limited moat, watch carefully |
| **Below 10%** | Concerning — may destroy value over time |

*S&P 500 average ROIC: ~9-10%*

**Two paths to high ROIC:**
1. **High margins** (pricing power) — Apple, Visa
2. **High turnover** (efficiency) — Costco, Walmart

Both work; understand which path the company follows.

**The compounding machine:**
```
Intrinsic Value Growth = ROIC × Reinvestment Rate
```
A business earning 20% ROIC that reinvests 50% of earnings grows intrinsic value at 10% annually before any multiple expansion.

### Intrinsic Value Estimation

Buffett's approach: "Intrinsic value is the present value of the stream of cash that's going to be generated by any financial asset between now and doomsday. And that's easy to say and impossible to figure."

**Key principles:**

1. **Use ranges, not point estimates** — "I'd rather be approximately right than precisely wrong"
2. **Multiple methods for cross-checking** — DCF, earnings power value, asset-based
3. **Conservative assumptions** — Use realistic growth rates, not optimistic projections
4. **Scenario analysis** — Conservative, base, optimistic cases

**Discount rate guidance:**
- Buffett thinks "in terms of the long-term government rate"
- Add 3-4% risk premium for typical investments
- Higher rates for more uncertain businesses
- Reject CAPM and beta-based approaches

**When intrinsic value is "unknowable":**
- Cash flows depend on unpredictable technology outcomes
- Business model is in rapid flux
- Competitive dynamics are changing faster than you can analyze
- If you can't estimate within 30%, the margin of safety is meaningless

## Example Analyses

### Example 1: Clear Favorable Case

```
## COST (Costco) Analysis

### The Evidence

**Financial Health**
- 15 consecutive years of revenue growth
- Operating margins stable at 3.4% (intentionally low — membership model)
- Net debt negative (more cash than debt)
- FCF conversion consistently strong

**Competitive Position**
- 90%+ membership renewal rate (extremely sticky)
- Largest warehouse club globally
- Buying power advantage compounds with scale

**Valuation**
- P/E: 52x (elevated vs market, but typical for COST)
- 5-year avg P/E: 40x
- Premium reflects quality, but historically reverts

### Bull Case
- Membership model creates recurring, predictable revenue
- 90%+ renewal rate is exceptional customer loyalty evidence
- International expansion runway (underpenetrated in Asia, Europe)
- Inflation-resistant value proposition strengthens in uncertain times
- Management conservative, shareholder-aligned (special dividends)

### Bear Case
- 52x P/E leaves little room for disappointment
- Same-store sales growth moderating from pandemic highs
- Amazon/Walmart competitive pressure on general merchandise
- Labor costs rising, may pressure margins
- Premium valuation assumes continued execution

### Value Investing Lens

**Circle of Competence:** High — retail business model is straightforward.
Pay annual fee, get access to bulk goods at thin margins. Economics are clear.

**Moat Assessment:** Strong evidence of durability
- Membership renewal rates (90%+) demonstrate switching costs
- Scale advantages in purchasing difficult to replicate
- Real estate portfolio (owned, not leased) is underappreciated asset

**Management Quality:** Excellent track record
- Founder-led culture maintained post-transition
- Capital allocation disciplined (special dividends vs empire building)
- Employee treatment creates operational advantage

**Intrinsic Value Range:** $750 - $950
- Conservative (35x normalized earnings): $750
- Base (42x reflecting quality): $850
- Optimistic (50x premium sustained): $950

**Margin of Safety:** Limited at $920 (current price at high end of range)

### Unanswered Questions
- Will premium multiple compress if growth slows further?
- How much international expansion is already priced in?
- Can membership fee increases continue without churn?

### Assessment

**Position on spectrum:** Favorable lean

**Reasoning:** Business quality is exceptional with clear, durable competitive
advantages. Management track record is strong. However, valuation provides
limited margin of safety — you're paying a fair price for a great business,
not a bargain price.

**Conviction level:** Medium — business quality high conviction,
but entry valuation introduces uncertainty about forward returns

**What would change this:**
- Upgrade: Price decline to $750 range (creates margin of safety)
- Downgrade: Membership renewal decline below 88%, margin compression

**Note:** Growth-oriented investors comfortable with quality premiums may view
this more favorably. Deep value investors seeking margin of safety will likely
wait for better entry points.
```

### Example 2: Genuinely Mixed Case

```
## TSLA (Tesla) Analysis

### The Evidence

**Financial Health**
- Revenue CAGR 50%+ over 5 years (exceptional growth)
- Automotive margins compressed from 25% to 17% (price cuts)
- $26B cash, $2B debt (fortress balance sheet)
- FCF positive but volatile with capex cycles

**Competitive Position**
- EV market share declining as competition intensifies
- Brand strength remains, especially in US market
- Manufacturing efficiency (Austin, Berlin) improving
- Charging network is genuine competitive advantage

**Valuation**
- Forward P/E: 65x
- P/S: 8x (vs auto industry <1x)
- Priced for significant non-auto revenue (energy, FSD)

### Bull Case
- Global EV transition creates multi-decade tailwind
- Energy storage business growing 100%+ (underappreciated)
- FSD technology, if realized, transforms unit economics
- Manufacturing expertise and cost position improving
- Musk's execution track record despite controversy
- Brand loyalty creates pricing power

### Bear Case
- 65x forward P/E requires exceptional execution across multiple bets
- FSD timeline repeatedly missed — regulatory path unclear
- Chinese EV competition intensifying (BYD, NIO, Xiaomi)
- Legacy automakers scaling EV production (Ford, GM, VW)
- Margin pressure may be structural, not cyclical
- Key person risk (Musk distraction, reputation)
- Regulatory tailwinds (EV credits) diminishing

### Value Investing Lens

**Circle of Competence:** Challenging
The bull case requires assessing: (1) autonomous driving technology timelines,
(2) robotics potential, (3) energy storage market evolution. These are difficult
to evaluate with confidence.

**Moat Assessment:** Mixed evidence
- Brand and charging network: durable advantages with evidence
- Manufacturing: improving but replicable
- Technology: unproven at scale, timeline uncertain

**Management Quality:** Complicated
- Execution on production scaling: exceptional
- Capital allocation: reasonable (no dilution, minimal debt)
- Focus and attention: legitimately uncertain given other ventures

**Intrinsic Value Range:** Wide dispersion reflecting uncertainty
- Conservative (auto-only, compressed multiples): $120
- Base (moderate energy/FSD value): $250
- Optimistic (significant FSD/robotaxi value): $500+

**Margin of Safety:** Negative at $380 vs conservative case

### Unanswered Questions
- When (if ever) does FSD achieve regulatory approval for robotaxi?
- Will energy storage scale profitably?
- Is margin compression cyclical or structural?
- How much automotive market share loss is acceptable?

### Assessment

**Position on spectrum:** Mixed/Neutral for traditional value investors

**Reasoning:** The evidence genuinely supports both bullish and bearish
conclusions depending on which assumptions you make about unknowable future
outcomes. The business has real strengths, but the valuation requires success
in areas we cannot confidently assess.

**Conviction level:** Low — not because the business is bad, but because
the thesis depends on outcomes outside our analytical confidence

**What would change this:**
- More favorable: Price decline creating margin of safety ($150-180 range),
  or concrete FSD regulatory approval
- Less favorable: Continued margin compression, market share erosion
  accelerating beyond EV transition

**Note:** This is a case where reasonable, intelligent investors can reach
opposite conclusions. Growth investors with tech expertise and higher risk
tolerance may see compelling opportunity. Traditional value investors will
likely pass due to valuation and circle of competence concerns. Neither
view is obviously wrong.
```

**Financial Health**

**Position on spectrum:** Strong conviction unfavorable

## Intellectual Honesty Principles

### What We Acknowledge

**Limitations we're explicit about:**
- Valuation is estimation, not precision — we provide ranges, not point targets
- The future is uncertain — macro conditions, competitive dynamics, and management decisions can change
- Our circle of competence has boundaries — some businesses are harder to evaluate
- Timing is beyond our ability — we assess value, not when the market will recognize it

**When we say "we don't know":**
- Technology trajectories requiring specialized expertise
- Regulatory outcomes dependent on political processes
- Consumer behavior shifts without historical precedent
- Short-term price movements

### Questions We Always Ask

1. **What's the evidence?** (Not: what do we want to believe?)
2. **What are we assuming?** (And are those assumptions testable?)
3. **What would prove us wrong?** (And are we watching for it?)
4. **Where might we be biased?** (Recent performance, narrative appeal, etc.)
5. **Would Buffett understand this?** (Circle of competence check)

## Behavioral Discipline

### Temperament Over Intelligence

Buffett's most consistent message: "Investing is not a game where the guy with the 160 IQ beats the guy with 130 IQ. Once you have ordinary intelligence, what you need is the temperament to control the urges that get other people into trouble."

**The emotional qualities that matter:**
- **Patience** — "The stock market is designed to transfer money from the active to the patient"
- **Discipline** — Stick to your process when it's hardest
- **Emotional stability** — "You have to have an inner peace about your decisions"
- **Independence** — Think for yourself, not with the crowd

**Why smart people fail at investing:**
- Overtrading based on news and noise
- Panic selling during downturns
- Chasing trends due to fear of missing out
- Overcomplicating simple decisions
- Overconfidence in their ability to predict short-term moves

### Contrarian Action: When to Be Greedy

Buffett's famous maxim: "Be fearful when others are greedy, and greedy when others are fearful."

**Executing contrarian action requires:**
- Pre-existing research on what you'd buy and at what price
- Cash reserves to deploy during panic
- Psychological fortitude to act when it feels wrong
- Conviction based on fundamentals, not narrative

**Historical context:** During the 2008 financial crisis, Buffett deployed $14.5 billion while others fled, including investments in Goldman Sachs and Bank of America that generated billions in profits. This only works if you've done the work before the crisis.

### Long-term Holding: Time as Ally

Buffett on holding periods: "When we own portions of outstanding businesses with outstanding managements, our favorite holding period is forever."

**Benefits of long-term holding:**
- Tax efficiency through deferred capital gains
- Allows compounding to work uninterrupted
- Red
uces transaction costs
- Forces better initial selection
- Eliminates need to time re-entry

**Berkshire's turnover:** Approximately 2% annually — implying a 50-year average holding period. Compare to typical mutual fund turnover of 100% (1-2 year holding period).

**When "hold forever" doesn't apply:**
- The original thesis has broken (not just temporary setback)
- Management has fundamentally changed for the worse
- Competitive position has permanently deteriorated
- A significantly better opportunity requires the capital
- Valuation has become dangerously stretched

### The Punch Card Model

Buffett's mental model: "I could improve your ultimate financial welfare by giving you a ticket with only 20 slots in it so that you had 20 punches—representing all the investments that you got to make in a lifetime."

**The implication:**
- Every investment decision should be weighed heavily
- Be willing to pass on good opportunities waiting for great ones
- Load up on your highest-conviction ideas
- Quality of decisions matters more than quantity

Berkshire's concentration reflects this: top 5 holdings represent over 64% of the portfolio. "You don't need 20 great ideas. Four or five will probably do it over time."

### Independent Thought: Your Facts, Your Reasoning

Buffett credits Graham with the foundational insight: "You're neither right nor wrong because people agree with you. You're right because your facts are right and your reasoning is right—that's the only thing that makes you right."

**Protecting your independence:**
- Limit exposure to financial media noise
- Don't anchor to analyst price targets
- Ignore what the stock price did yesterday
- Be skeptical of consensus narratives
- Verify claims with primary sources (10-Ks, earnings calls)

**The institutional imperative warning:** Most managers feel compelled to do what everyone else is doing. Resist this urge — it leads to buying high and selling low.

## Portfolio Management

### Tracking Positions

```
"Add 100 shares of AAPL at $175 to my portfolio"
"Show my portfolio with current P&L"
"What's my overall risk exposure?"
```

### Reviewing Holdings

For each position, periodically reassess:
- Has the original thesis changed?
- Is the evidence still supportive?
- Has the margin of safety expanded or compressed?
- Are there better opportunities for the capital?

### Position Sizing Philosophy

- **Higher conviction + clearer evidence** → larger position (but diversified)
- **Mixed evidence or lower conviction** → smaller position or avoid
- **Correlation awareness** — don't concentrate in correlated bets

## Key Reminders

### Before Analysis

1. **Seek evidence, not confirmation** — Actively look for disconfirming data
2. **Acknowledge uncertainty** — Comfort with "I don't know" is a feature
3. **Remember the framework** — Buffett's principles are guides, not laws

### During Analysis

1. **Present both sides** — Bull and bear cases deserve fair treatment
2. **Be specific about assumptions** — What must be true for thesis to work?
3. **Quantify when possible** — Ranges > point estimates > hand-waving

### After Analysis

1. **Match conviction to evidence clarity** — Don't overstate confidence
2. **Acknowledge reasonable disagreement** — Smart people can see it differently
3. **Define what would change the view** — Make it falsifiable

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Value Traps: When "Cheap" Isn't Undervalued

A value trap looks cheap but is cheap for good reasons. Seth Klarman warns: "Value traps are cheap for a reason—perhaps an inept and entrenched management, a poor history of capital allocation, or assets whose value is in inexorable decline."

**Red flags for value traps:**

| Warning Sign | What It Suggests |
|--------------|------------------|
| Declining revenue for 3+ years | Secular not cyclical problems |
| Market share erosion despite price cuts | Lost competitive position |
| High debt with low-quality assets | Equity may be worthless |
| Management turnover or activist pressure | Internal problems |
| No clear path to competitive differentiation | The moat has collapsed |

**The key question:** Ask "Why is this cheap?" before "How cheap is this?"

### Over-Concentration Without Research

Buffett concentrates heavily — but backs each position with decades of research and deep expertise. Common mistakes:

- Putting 20%+ in a stock based on a tip or headline
- Concentrating based on recent performance (recency bias)
- Not understanding the business model well enough to hold through volatility
- Confusing conviction with research depth

**Match concentration to your research depth.** If you can't spend 10+ hours researching a position, consider limiting it to 5% or less.

### Ignoring Circle of Competence

The most dangerous investments are in companies you *think* you understand but don't. Warning signs:

- Relying on others' analysis rather than your own
- Unable to explain the business model simply
- The thesis requires predicting technology outcomes
- You don't know what would prove you wrong

**Buffett avoided technology for decades** — not because he thought tech was bad, but because it was outside his circle. He only invested in Apple after years of observing consumer behavior and brand loyalty, characteristics he could evaluate regardless of the technology label.

### "Hold Forever" Misapplied

"Our favorite holding period is forever" applies to wonderful businesses. It doesn't mean:

- Ignoring deteriorating fundamentals
- Holding through thesis-breaking changes
- Never reassessing your position
- Emotional attachment to a stock

**When to sell (despite the "forever" philosophy):**
- Competitive advantage has permanently eroded
- Management has made capital allocation errors
- A better opportunity requires the capital
- Valuation has become dangerously disconnected from value

Buffett has sold many positions: Tesco (mistake acknowledged), IBM (thesis didn't work), airlines (industry economics changed). "Hold forever" assumes the business remains wonderful.

### Expecting Buffett's Returns Without His Advantages

Individual investors cannot replicate:
- Access to negotiated private deals (Goldman Sachs preferred stock)
- Insurance float providing zero-cost leverage
- Reputation that attracts opportunities
- Scale providing management influence
- Corporate tax structure advantages

**What you CAN replicate:**
- Rigorous business analysis
- Circle of competence discipline
- Long-term holding periods
- Contrarian action during panic
- Behavioral discipline

Focus on principles, not exact methods. Your advantage as a smaller investor: access to smaller opportunities institutions cannot touch.

### The Buffett Checklist

- [ ] Do I understand this business?
- [ ] Is there evidence of a durable moat?
- [ ] Is management aligned with shareholders?
- [ ] Does margin of safety exist at current price?
- [ ] Would I be comfortable owning this for 10 years?
- [ ] What could go wrong?

### Moat Quick Assessment

| Moat Type | Quick Test |
|-----------|-----------|
| Brand | Do customers ask for it by name? |
| Network | Would leaving hurt the user? |
| Switching | What would changing require? |
| Cost | Can competitors match with capital? |
| Regulatory | Is the barrier legal or economic? |

### Management Red Flags

| Red Flag | What to Check |
|----------|---------------|
| Excessive compensation | CEO pay vs. median employee, vs. peers |
| Empire building | Acquisitions at high multiples, unrelated diversification |
| Spin and blame | Annual letters that avoid discussing failures |
| Low insider ownership | Management with no skin in the game |
| Aggressive accounting | Frequent "non-GAAP" adjustments, revenue recognition changes |

### Key Valuation Metrics

| Metric | What It Shows | Caution |
|--------|---------------|---------|
| P/E | Price relative to earnings | Can be manipulated by accounting |
| P/FCF | Price relative to actual cash | Better than P/E for capital-intensive |
| EV/EBITDA | Enterprise value to operating profit | Ignores capex needs |
| P/B | Price relative to book value | Book value may be outdated |
| ROIC | Returns on invested capital | Below 10% signals weak moat |
