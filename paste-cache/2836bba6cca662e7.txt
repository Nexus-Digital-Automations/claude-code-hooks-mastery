If you want to “play it safe” for both general health *and* microbiome health (and you don’t have IBS), the most defensible blacklist is actually pretty short. The key is to blacklist the additives with (a) strong human outcome evidence or (b) fairly consistent gut-barrier / dysbiosis evidence at real-world exposures, and not chase every scary-sounding ingredient.

Here’s the practical blacklist I’d use.

- **Partially hydrogenated oils (industrial trans fat)** — strongest evidence of harm, no upside. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3551118/)
- **Brominated vegetable oil (BVO)** — bioaccumulation concerns and FDA action; easy to avoid because it’s basically only in certain beverages. [cnn](https://www.cnn.com/2024/07/02/health/fda-brominated-vegetable-oil-ban-wellness)
- **Potassium bromate** — IARC classifies it as “possibly carcinogenic to humans” (Group 2B) with sufficient animal evidence; very reasonable to avoid when you can. [inchem](https://www.inchem.org/documents/iarc/vol73/73-17.html)
- **Titanium dioxide (E171)** — EFSA concluded it’s no longer considered safe as a food additive because genotoxicity couldn’t be ruled out and it can accumulate despite low absorption. [efsa.europa](https://www.efsa.europa.eu/en/news/titanium-dioxide-e171-no-longer-considered-safe-when-used-food-additive)
- **Emulsifiers most linked to gut-barrier disruption**:  
  - **Polysorbate 80** (and, broadly, “emulsifier-heavy” ultra-processed foods) — strongest mechanistic/microbiome signal among common additives. [nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s42003-024-06224-3)
  - **Carrageenan** — more mixed than polysorbate 80, but enough gut-inflammation concern that “play it safe” avoidance is reasonable. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9331555/)
- **Artificial sweeteners with the most consistent microbiome disruption signal**:  
  - **Sucralose** — repeatedly flagged in reviews as disruptive to microbiome composition/function (even though regulators consider it safe for traditional toxicology endpoints). [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12025785/)
  - **Saccharin** — similar logic: not a “cancer” blacklist anymore, but a “microbiome caution” sweetener if you’re optimizing gut ecology. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6363527/)

Then I’d add two “food-based” blacklist items that matter more than many additives:

- **Processed meats (especially nitrite-cured)** — IARC classifies processed meat consumption as carcinogenic to humans (Group 1) with sufficient evidence for colorectal cancer; if you’re playing it safe, this is a bigger lever than most additive lists. [who](https://www.who.int/news-room/questions-and-answers/item/cancer-carcinogenicity-of-the-consumption-of-red-meat-and-processed-meat)
- **Ultra-processed foods as a category** — independent of any one additive, higher ultra-processed exposure is consistently associated with worse health outcomes in systematic reviews/meta-analyses, and it’s also where you get most emulsifiers and sweeteners. [bmj](https://www.bmj.com/content/384/bmj-2023-077310)
