If your problem is multicollinearity with CAPE, the practical rule is: don’t add *another* long-horizon “market is expensive vs fundamentals” ratio (Buffett indicator, Tobin’s q, price-to-sales, market cap / GVA, etc.). Those tend to be highly correlated with CAPE because they’re all different lenses on the same underlying latent factor: broad equity valuation. (Your 88% CAPE–Buffett correlation is completely plausible for that reason.)

Metrics that are typically *less* correlated with CAPE fall into “rates/discounting,” “risk appetite/credit,” and “macro conditions/liquidity” buckets:

- Real rates / term structure: use 10‑year TIPS real yield (or a long real yield proxy) rather than nominal yields; this moves with bond market pricing and monetary re equities and treats credit valuation/spreads as a distinct driver of forward-looking returns. [lseg](https://www.lseg.com/content/dam/ftse-russell/en_us/documents/research/valuation-matters-us-high-yield-and-equities.pdf)

- Economic slack / output gap / unemployment gap: these are cycle-position variables; the Morningstar summary of the Murphy–Alsalman–Souropanis work reports that deviations from the earnings-yield/TIPS baseline are related to “economic slack,” i.e., the model underestimates returns when slack is high and overestimates when the economy is running hot. [morningstar](https://www.morningstar.com/markets/this-simple-metric-could-predict-future-stock-market-returns)

- Monetary policy / liquidity variables (money supply growth, policy changes): these can move expected returns via discount rates and risk-taking even when CAPE is unchanged; the same summary reports links between deviations and monetary policy changes and money growth over shorter horizons. [morningstar](https://www.morningstar.com/markets/this-simple-metric-could-predict-future-stock-market-returns)

- Consumption–wealth style predictors (e.g., \(cay\)): these are constructed from macro aggregates (consumption, labor income, asset wealth) and are not just another price multiple, so they’re often materially less correlated with CAPE (though they’re harder to compute and can be revised). NBER work has studied these as predictors of excess stock returns. [nber](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w9605/w9605.pdf)

Two concrete “low-correlation to CAPE” combos people actually test are (1) CAPE + real rate (or CAPE + term spread), and (2) CAPE + a credit spread (HY OAS or BAA–AAA), because the second variable is trying to capture discount-rate/risk-premium conditions rather than “how expensive are stocks.”
