# The US Will Choose Inflation: Fiscal Dominance and the Coming Policy Response

**The United States will almost certainly choose an inflationary policy response in the next recession.** With debt-to-GDP at 124% and deficits running at $2+ trillion annually, history, institutional incentives, and expert consensus all point toward financial repression—tolerating inflation above target to erode debt in real terms—rather than deflation. Former Treasury Secretary Janet Yellen warned in January 2026 that "the preconditions for fiscal dominance are clearly strengthening," while Fed Chair Powell's continued commitment to the 2% target faces mounting structural pressure. For investors, this suggests a bearish posture on long-duration Treasuries, tactical opportunities in inverse Treasury ETFs like TMV, and strategic allocation to gold as a financial repression hedge.

---

## History's verdict: governments never choose deflation

The historical record is unambiguous. When advanced economies face unsustainable debt burdens with domestic-currency obligations, they choose inflation over deflation **virtually every time**. The mechanisms—financial repression, surprise inflation, negative real interest rates—constitute what economists call a "soft default" that avoids formal restructuring while systematically transferring wealth from creditors to debtors.

The post-World War II United States provides the clearest template. Debt-to-GDP peaked at **106% in 1946** and fell to just **23% by 1974**—an 83 percentage point decline. An IMF Working Paper by Acalin and Ball (2024) found that in a counterfactual scenario without interest rate distortions, debt would have declined only to 74% by 1974, not 23%. The difference came from financial repression: the Fed pegged long-term bond rates at 2.5% from 1942-1951, while Regulation Q capped private sector rates. Real interest rates were negative in most years, with average real returns on Treasury debt at **-0.3% annually** during 1945-1980. Reinhart and Sbrancia's research found that roughly **60% of years** between 1945 and 1980 were "liquidation years" with negative real rates.

Britain's experience was even more dramatic. Post-war debt peaked at approximately **250% of GDP**—more than double current US levels—yet fell to 50% by the 1970s without formal restructuring. The UK Office for Budget Responsibility found that negative real interest rates persisted in **24 of 30 years** from 1946-1976, with inflation averaging 6.5% while interest rates remained suppressed. Approximately 60% of debt reduction came from growth exceeding real interest rates.

The contrast with Japan is instructive. Japan's experience—deflation and economic stagnation despite debt reaching 260% of GDP—is often cited as an alternative model. But Japan's deflation was an accident of demographics and policy errors, not a deliberate choice. The result has been devastating: nominal GDP fell from $5.55 trillion (1995) to $4.27 trillion (2025), while Japan's share of world GDP collapsed from 17.8% to 3.6%. IMF research confirms that deflation **increased** Japan's real debt burden because nominal GDP contracted while debt remained fixed. No government voluntarily pursues this outcome.

## Current officials signal growing fiscal dominance concerns

The most significant policy statement came from Janet Yellen at Brookings on January 6, 2026. In what amounted to a warning to her successors, Yellen explicitly addressed fiscal dominance: "Fiscal dominance refers to a situation where the government's fiscal position—its deficits and debt—puts such pressure on its financing needs that monetary policy becomes subordinate to those needs." She acknowledged that "by the standards of the fiscal-dominance literature, I would agree with Chair Powell that the United States is not in a fiscal-dominance regime today," but warned that "the preconditions for fiscal dominance are clearly strengthening."

Yellen cited CBO projections showing debt rising from 100% to over **150% of GDP** within three decades, with net interest costs currently at **19% of revenue and 3.2% of GDP**, projected to reach 28% of revenue and 5.4% of GDP. The overall federal deficit is running at roughly **6% of GDP**—a level "never before realized except during wars and recessions." Her conclusion was stark: "The temptation to rely on inflation or financial repression to reduce the debt burden will surely grow."

Fed Chair Jerome Powell has consistently maintained commitment to the 2% inflation target. At Jackson Hole in August 2025, he declared: "Anchored expectations are critical to everything we do, and we remain fully committed to the 2 percent target today." The Fed even tightened its framework, dropping the "average inflation targeting" language that had allowed intentional overshoots. However, Powell has simultaneously acknowledged fiscal reality, stating in February 2024 that "the debt is growing faster than the economy" and calling for an "adult conversation among elected officials about getting the federal government back on a sustainable fiscal path."

The operational picture reveals tension between rhetoric and reality. The Fed ended quantitative tightening in December 2025 and resumed Treasury purchases, albeit ostensibly "solely for the purpose of maintaining an ample supply of reserves." Meanwhile, Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent has shifted issuance toward short-term bills—from 20% to 23-25% of the debt stack—to take advantage of lower short-term yields and reduce interest costs. This maturity shortening reduces immediate costs but dramatically increases rollover risk, making the government more vulnerable to rate spikes.

## Institutional incentives point decisively toward inflation

The Federal Reserve's dual mandate—maximum employment and price stability—creates asymmetric incentives that favor accommodation in recessions. Historical analysis by the Congressional Research Service shows the Fed prioritizes "whichever mandate is most acutely violated." In a recession with high debt but subdued inflation, employment concerns would dominate. Only if inflation expectations become unanchored would price stability take precedence—as demonstrated in 2022-2023.

Political economy research reveals voters respond differently to inflation versus unemployment. Eurasia Group examined 57 inflation shocks since 1970 and found government turnover in 58% of cases, with elections during or within two years of inflation shocks leading to government change in roughly **three out of four instances**. However, deflation imposes concentrated pain on debtors while causing mass unemployment—political suicide for any government. As Reinhart and Rogoff note: "Given that deficit reduction usually involves highly unpopular expenditure reductions and/or tax increases, authorities seeking to reduce outstanding debts may find the stealthier financial repression tax more politically palatable."

The fiscal dominance mechanism operates through what Sargent and Wallace called "unpleasant monetarist arithmetic" in their foundational 1981 paper. If fiscal authorities refuse to adjust deficits, tighter monetary policy increases government interest expense. Without fiscal adjustment, this must eventually be monetized—leading to higher future inflation. The St. Louis Fed explains: "A tighter monetary policy ends up increasing the interest expense of debt issuance. And if the fiscal authority is unwilling to curtail the rate of debt issuance, the added interest expense must be monetized." With interest payments on federal debt nearly doubling over three fiscal years to approximately **$1 trillion in FY2025**, this dynamic is already visible.

Expert consensus has shifted notably toward expecting elevated inflation. Olivier Blanchard stated at a Peterson Institute debate that "I wouldn't be surprised if the Fed said, okay, you've convinced us. Let's go to three. I suspect what the Fed will say is, well, let's have a range between 2 and 3." John Cochrane, the leading fiscal theory of the price level economist, argues the Fed cannot control inflation without fiscal support: "Inflation breaks out when there is more overall government debt than people think the government will repay by its future excess of taxes over spending." Goldman Sachs Asset Management's 2026 outlook predicts "the next economic cycle will be characterized by higher inflation, elevated interest rates and heightened macroeconomic volatility."

## Financial repression faces modern constraints but remains the likeliest tool

Carmen Reinhart, whose research with Sbrancia documented the post-war financial repression era, has acknowledged that modern conditions differ from 1945-1980. "Going back to 1900, there have only been four periods of sustained, negative real short-term interest rates: World War I, World War II, the 1970s, and the last and longest was following the global financial crisis," she noted in a 2025 Richmond Fed interview. "With the post-COVID inflationary spike, we seem to be heading into an era with more uncertainty and higher levels of volatility, so I think that sort of mild financial repression will be much more of a challenge to implement."

The barriers are substantial: global capital mobility (versus post-WWII capital controls), independent central banks with inflation mandates, sophisticated financial markets that can arbitrage repression, and political/legal constraints on directing institutional investments. Yet some forms of financial repression have already occurred. Real interest rates turned negative during much of 2020-2022. And as Reinhart-Sbrancia documented, post-WWII financial repression allowed the US and UK to liquidate debt through negative real rates amounting to **3-4% of GDP annually**—a powerful mechanism even if imperfectly implemented.

The key uncertainty is political pressure on Fed independence. Multiple sources document unprecedented pressure in 2025, with President Trump demanding rate cuts to reduce debt service costs. Yellen warned this represents "the voice of fiscal dominance—the idea that monetary policy must serve fiscal constraints, not price stability." The Council on Foreign Relations noted "Trump's public criticism has reached new heights, threatening the independence of the Federal Reserve." Historical precedents—Turkey's central bank subjugation leading to 75% inflation, Argentina's chronic inflation from repeated independence compromises—illustrate the stakes.

## Investment implications favor gold and short duration, not long bonds

For investors, the inflation scenario implies significant portfolio adjustments. Long-duration Treasuries face a structural headwind: if real rates remain positive but inflation persists above target, long-bond holders suffer ongoing real losses. Jeffrey Gundlach of DoubleLine ($90B+ AUM) has been explicit: "There's an awareness now that the long-term Treasury bond is not a legitimate flight-to-quality asset... A reckoning is coming." He recommends cash or 3-5 year Treasuries over 30-year bonds.

The term premium—the extra yield investors demand for duration risk—has already expanded to **0.8% in January 2025**, the highest since 2011. The St. Louis Fed notes this accounts for "more than half of the recent rise in 10-year Treasury yields." State Street warns the premium could rise further as "US exceptionalism wanes" and "investors demand a higher risk premium to fund the country's deficits at very long maturities."

TMV (Direxion Daily 20+ Year Treasury Bear 3X) offers tactical exposure to rising long-term yields. The ETF returned **151% in 2022** during aggressive Fed rate hikes. In a sustained inflation scenario with rising yields, TMV would profit—but leverage decay makes extended holding periods dangerous. This is a tactical tool, not a strategic allocation.

Gold stands out as the primary beneficiary of financial repression. During post-2008 QE, gold prices rose **over 100%** from 2008 to 2011. Gundlach predicts gold reaching $4,000, citing "waning investor confidence in central bankers" and ongoing central bank purchases. China's gold reserves surged from 1,948 tonnes (2019) to 2,280 tonnes (2024). As Morningstar notes: "To avoid the effects of financial repression, investors should focus on real assets such as gold, precious metals, real estate, or stocks."

But March 2020 provided a warning: the Treasury market experienced "severe stress and illiquidity" despite being the safe haven asset, with yields briefly rising during peak panic. The stock-bond correlation has shifted from -0.72 (November 2024) to +0.39 (January 2025)—positive correlation means bonds no longer hedge stocks effectively when "inflation driven by supply shocks" dominates.

## Conclusion: The historical playbook will repeat

The thesis that US fiscal space is exhausted leads to a clear analytical conclusion: **the US will choose financial repression over deflation**. History provides no examples of advanced economies deliberately pursuing deflation when facing domestic-currency debt burdens. The post-WWII template—tolerating inflation above target, keeping real rates low or negative, allowing nominal GDP growth to outpace debt growth—remains the path of least resistance.

The key novel insight is timing and degree. Unlike 1945-1980, the US cannot easily impose capital controls or direct institutional investment. Fed independence, while threatened, still constrains outright monetization. The most likely scenario is therefore **moderate financial repression**: inflation persisting at 2.5-4% while policymakers maintain the fiction of targeting 2%, real rates kept low but not consistently negative, and gradual erosion of debt burdens over a decade or more rather than the rapid post-war adjustment.

For investors, this argues against the traditional 60/40 portfolio allocation to long-duration bonds. Strategic exposure to inflation hedges—gold, TIPS at current real yields of 1.9-2.1%, shorter-duration fixed income—and tactical willingness to profit from yield spikes through instruments like TMV represent the appropriate positioning. The question is not whether the US chooses inflation, but how skillfully policymakers manage the transition and whether Fed independence survives intact.
