The sectors best poised to profit from a water shortage are the ones that (a) get **mandated** spending, (b) enable **efficiency** (less water per unit output), or (c) create **new supply** (reuse/desal). Those forces tend to push capital toward utilities + “picks-and-shovels” industrials rather than consumer brands. [thedriller](https://www.thedriller.com/articles/93753-global-desalination-and-water-reuse-markets-enter-historic-growth-phase)

## 1) Regulated water & wastewater utilities
When scarcity and aging pipes force upgrades, utilities typically respond with higher capex (pipe replacement, treatment upgrades, storage) and then seek rate increases to recover costs, which can translate into steady rate-base growth over long periods. [energycap](https://www.energycap.com/blog/the-high-cost-of-old-pipes/)
They’re “best poised” because spending is non-discretionary: you can delay iPhone upgrades, but you can’t defer safe drinking water and basic sanitation indefinitely. [deloitte](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/insights/topics/economy/issues-by-the-numbers/us-aging-water-infrastructure-investment-opportunities.html)

## 2) Water infrastructure replacement (pipes, valves, pumps, rehab)
A big driver of the water “crisis” is simply that much of the underground network is reaching end of life; Deloitte cites an AWWA estimate of at least **$1 trillion** to restore underground pipes over ~25 years (not even counting new infrastructure). [deloitte](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/insights/topics/economy/issues-by-the-numbers/us-aging-water-infrastructure-investment-opportunities.html)
That kind of multi-decade replacement cycle benefits suppliers of distribution hardware, repair technologies, trenchless rehab, and pumping systems—especially as utilities move from “fix breaks” to proactive replacement programs. [energycap](https://www.energycap.com/blog/the-high-cost-of-old-pipes/)

## 3) Industrial water treatment & recycling (including ZLD)
Industries (chemicals, food & beverage, pharma, semis, data centers) are being pushed to treat, recycle, and monitor water more aggressively as scarcity and discharge regulations tighten. [breakthroughgroup](https://breakthroughgroup.com/market_watch/industrial-water-treatment-market/)
One industry forecast pegs industrial water treatment reaching about **$80.5B** by 2035 (5.3% CAGR), explicitly citing water scarcity + regulation as demand drivers. [breakthroughgroup](https://breakthroughgroup.com/market_watch/industrial-water-treatment-market/)

## 4) Desalination & water reuse (new supply creation)
When natural freshwater sources aren’t enough, utilities and governments expand supply via desalination and wastewater reuse. [openknowledge.worldbank](https://openknowledge.worldbank.org/entities/publication/e84de217-1303-4f50-b940-af2cfc28194d)
Global Water Intelligence/IDRA reporting indicates desalination capacity is up ~40% since 2020 and reuse capacity up ~52% since 2020, with **190+ desalination projects contracted in 2024–2025** and **~200 reuse projects recorded since 2022**, which is exactly the kind of multi-year project pipeline that can feed sector growth. [thedriller](https://www.thedriller.com/articles/93753-global-desalination-and-water-reuse-markets-enter-historic-growth-phase)

## 5) Leak detection, smart metering, and “non‑revenue water” reduction
Cutting losses is often the cheapest “new supply.” Smart metering and analytics help utilities detect leaks faster, improve billing accuracy, and reduce non‑revenue water (NRW). [workongrid](https://www.workongrid.com/blog/a-guide-to-smart-water-metering-for-utilities)
One utility-focused source cites NRW around **30% of global water system input volume**, framing a large economic incentive for utilities to deploy smart meters and monitoring systems. [workongrid](https://www.workongrid.com/blog/a-guide-to-smart-water-metering-for-utilities)

## 6) Agricultural water efficiency (irrigation tech, precision ag)
Agriculture is a major water user, so scarcity tends to accelerate adoption of more efficient irrigation, scheduling, and fertigation systems to maintain yields with less water. [hortidaily](https://www.hortidaily.com/article/9791689/smart-irrigation-technology-advances-as-water-scarcity-tightens-its-grip/)
Smart irrigation providers report meaningful water savings (e.g., 25–40% reductions in some implementations), and even if individual claims vary, the direction of travel is clear: scarcity pushes growers toward automation and precision. [hortidaily](https://www.hortidaily.com/article/9791689/smart-irrigation-technology-advances-as-water-scarcity-tightens-its-grip/)

### Practical way to invest (sector mapping)
- “Safe/steady” water crisis exposure: **Water utilities + water infrastructure suppliers** (rate-base + replacement cycle). [energycap](https://www.energycap.com/blog/the-high-cost-of-old-pipes/)
- “Higher growth / more cyclical”: **Industrial water treatment + reuse/desal + smart metering/leak detection** (capex cycles, tech adoption). [thedriller](https://www.thedriller.com/articles/93753-global-desalination-and-water-reuse-markets-enter-historic-growth-phase)
- “High variance but huge TAM”: **Ag efficiency tech** (depends on farm economics, subsidies, and crop prices). [ers.usda](http://www.ers.usda.gov/topics/farm-practices-management/irrigation-water-use)
